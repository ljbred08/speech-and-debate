
# CONGRESSIONAL STOCK TRADING

## BY THE NUMBERS

### SUMMARY

Solutions for the vast array of conflicts of interest caused by congressional stock trading continue to proliferate, so it bears emphasizing why these reforms are necessary in the first place. While the STOCK Act provides for disclosure of stock transactions and prohibits insider trading, the law does not otherwise restrict the ownership and trading of stock by lawmakers. This means that members of Congress can own, buy, and sell individual stock even where that stock will conflict with their official duties.

To reduce the possibility of conflicts of interest, some members own stock through a qualified blind trust (“QBT”), invest only in widely held investment funds, or do not invest in the stock market at all. Many members, however, do own individual stock, which continues to pose risks of actual and perceived conflicts of interest. This fact sheet shows the landscape of stock ownership in the current 117ᵗʰ Congress, leading up to the push for reforming the STOCK Act.

## STOCK AND THE 117ᵀᴴ CONGRESS

Based on annual financial disclosures and blind trust reports filed in 2021, out of 535 total members of Congress,

* 284 (53%) own stock (223 Representatives, 61 Senators). Of those, 263 own both stocks and widely held investment funds.
* 212 (40%) own only widely held investment funds (mutual funds, ETFs, pensions) (174 Representatives, 38 Senators).
* 39 (7%) do not own stock or widely held investment funds (38 Representatives, 1 Senator).
* 10 (2%) have a QBT (4 Representatives, 6 Senator). Of those, all also own stocks or widely held investment funds that are not held in their QBT based on the most recent publicly available disclosures.

Ownership includes assets of the member, spouse, and dependent children, and is based on accuracy and clarity of members’ disclosures filed in 2021

## ABOUT THE CAMPAIGN LEGAL CENTER

At Campaign Legal Center (CLC), we are advancing democracy through law, fighting for every American’s right to participate in the democratic process. CLC uses tactics such as litigation, policy advocacy, communications, and partnerships to win victories that result in a more transparent, accountable, and inclusive democracy. Our long-term goal is a government responsive to the people.
